# Tone of voice

> **Owner:** Content team
> **Last updated:** 2026-04-17
> **Status:** Live
> **Source:** Figma Brand Kit SOT — Tone of voice section + Notion Wiki

Tone of voice defines how Qonto speaks to the world. It builds on our brand strategy to reflect our unique personality. These principles shape our voice, with practical guidance to help you speak in an authentically Qonto way.

Qonto's mission: to create the freedom for SMEs to succeed in business, and beyond.

---

## Principles

Four pillars define Qonto's voice across all languages. They must be present in every piece of communication.

### 1. To the point

We know our customers are time-poor, so we get straight to the point. No fluff, no BS.

This doesn't mean finding the shortest possible way of expressing something; rather, it's about finding the most efficient way to express it. Let's not use 30 words if we can find 15 better words that do the job.

This principle mirrors our product: our objective is to help our busy customers achieve their objectives as quickly as possible. It reflects our **delightfully efficient** behavior.

**We are:** Direct · Concise · Purposeful

**We are NOT:** Cold · Dismissive · Simplistic

### 2. Playful but polished

We delight our customers and bring a smile to their faces with copy that's **playful and sharp, warm and approachable**. This quality reflects our quick-witted and friendly yet professional personality.

We innovate with our words, just like we do with our product. Wit and charm come naturally, but we never let clever wordplay get in the way of clarity and purpose for our customers.

Being polished does not mean being intellectual or verbose: we impress our audience by making our message shine and sparkle, not by trying to set a record Scrabble score.

We prove we're reliable through our consistency and faultless attention to detail, but we also know how and when to add a playful twist or a friendly wink.

**We are:** Quick-witted · Confident · Friendly yet professional

**We are NOT:** Pretentious · Sloppy · Bland & unimaginative

### 3. In tune

Every customer is different, so we tailor our language to each of our audiences. We don't do "one-size-fits-all" copy.

Whether we're talking to Anna on TikTok, John on LinkedIn or Richard via email, we meet each of them on their wavelength and empathize with their respective experiences, so that what we say always resonates.

We need to understand them on a human level if we're to give them freedom in business, and beyond.

We're **unwaveringly attentive** to their needs, and use language that's familiar and accessible to each of them.

Internally, too, we're sensitive to the concerns of every Qontoer, and careful to use language that's inclusive.

**We are:** Astute · Inclusive · Relatable

**We are NOT:** Presumptuous · Generic · Obscure

### 4. At your service

We genuinely love to help businesses **get things done and succeed**. So, in our copy, the customer is the star of the show.

We don't lead them to success; we **discreetly but proactively enable** their journey. To reflect this, we are understated and avoid hyperbole and self-aggrandizing. We focus on the "you"; use of "we" is collective: it's us and the audience, together.

Beyond our product, entrepreneurs also need positivity in order to succeed. So we provide that too, framing our messaging in terms of **opportunities and possibilities**.

Business can be hard, so our unobtrusive and genuine support is **refreshingly welcoming**.

**We are:** Supportive · Understated · Proactive

**We are NOT:** Boastful · Pushy · Overfamiliar

## Rules — shared

### Non-negotiables

**Do:**
- Preserve meaning when rewriting or localizing
- Check approved terminology (glossary) before writing
- Be specific and concrete — prefer short bullets over long narratives
- Add placeholders when facts are missing (e.g. [benefit], [metric], [price])
- Frame around opportunities and possibilities, not problems

**Don't:**
- Invent facts, product claims, pricing, legal language, or metrics
- Use jargon, hype, or unverifiable superlatives
- Write like a traditional bank
- Try to impress with complexity — impress with clarity

### Self-check (before publishing)

- Clear, accurate, and actionable?
- Matches the 4 pillars?
- Correct glossary terms?
- No unverified claims?
- Would a busy business owner read this to the end?

### Localization

Qonto operates in France, Germany, Italy, Spain, and the Netherlands. Tone principles apply across all markets, but:

- Humor and wordplay may not translate — prioritize clarity over cleverness in non-native markets
- Formality levels vary by culture — German and Italian audiences may expect slightly more formal register
- Always have local native speakers review copy for cultural fit

## Rules — marketing

### Headlines and campaign copy

- Sentence case always
- Lead with the benefit, not the feature
- Speak to the reader directly ("you" / "your business")
- Be quick-witted and inventive — but never sacrifice clarity for wordplay
- Avoid superlatives and unverifiable claims
- Frame in terms of opportunities and possibilities

### Social media

- Conversational but not casual
- One clear point per post
- Adapt tone slightly per platform (TikTok ≠ LinkedIn ≠ Email) while staying recognizably Qonto

---

## Rules — product

### UI copy

- Be specific: "Save invoice" not "Submit"
- Use verbs that describe the outcome
- Keep labels to 1-3 words where possible
- "You" before "We" — frame around the user's need
- Reflect delightfully efficient behavior

### Error messages

- Say what happened (plain language)
- Say what the user can do about it
- Never blame the user
- Keep it brief — one short sentence for what happened, one for the next step

### Success and confirmation

- Acknowledge the action simply ("Transfer sent" not "Your transfer has been successfully submitted")
- Add context when useful ("Transfer sent. It should arrive by tomorrow.")

### Onboarding and empty states

- Guide, don't lecture
- One step at a time
- Turn empty screens into opportunities

---

## Negative cues (never do this)

- Corporate jargon: "leverage," "synergy," "streamline," "innovative"
- Traditional bank language: stiff, formal, impersonal
- Hype or unverifiable superlatives: "the best," "revolutionary"
- Trying too hard: forced humor, excessive exclamation marks
- Being pushy, overfamiliar, or authoritative
- Being pretentious, intellectual, or verbose
- Making obscure cultural references that don't translate
- Inventing facts or claims
- Being cold, dismissive, or simplistic under the guise of "concise"
- Bland, unimaginative, generic copy


## Examples

### Good: to the point

"Your transfer is on its way." — Clear, efficient, no filler. Delightfully efficient.

### Good: playful but polished

"All set. Your card is ready to use." — Warm personality without overdoing it. Shine and sparkle, not Scrabble score.

### Good: at your service

"That payment didn't go through. Here's what you can do." — User-first, no blame, actionable. Discreetly enabling.

### Good: in tune

"Starting a business is exciting (and a bit overwhelming). We're here to make the money part simple." — Meets the user on their wavelength. Unwaveringly attentive.

### Bad: traditional bank

"We hereby inform you that your transaction has been processed. Please allow 2-3 business days for settlement." — Stiff, impersonal, no personality.

### Bad: trying too hard

"OMG you just made your FIRST transfer!!! How exciting is that?! 🎉🎉🎉" — Forced, loud, not polished.

### Bad: jargon-heavy

"Leverage Qonto's innovative suite of financial management solutions to streamline your business operations." — Jargon, says nothing specific, could be any company.

### Bad: self-aggrandizing

"We are the leading business finance platform." — Boastful, "we" centered, unverifiable superlative.
